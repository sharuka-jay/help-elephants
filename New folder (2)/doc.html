Introduction And Current Situation

The Sri Lankan elephant is a subspecies of the Asian elephant that is only found in Sri Lanka. Since 1987, the IUCN has classified the Sri Lankan elephant as endangered. The Sri Lankan elephant population is now restricted to the dry zone in the north, east, and southeast of Sri Lanka. The average herd size is between 12 and 20 individuals. The oldest female elephant leads itÂ in the herd. As a result of construction and forest clearing, Elephants are pushed into smaller areas. In the first half of the 20th century, the Sri Lankan elephant population near around 7000 to 8000, and according to the DWC records, the current Sri Lankan elephant population is around 5000. In fact, some experts believe Sri Lankan elephants will be extinct in the wild within the next few decades.

The leading cause of death for elephants is clashing with villagers and farmers. The loss and destruction of their habitat have caused them to migrate through village areas. Elephants are also dying due to the high demand for ivory.

Destruction of crops, destruction of houses and damage to human lives are common problems in Sri Lanka. Due to this situation, the people in those villages resort to shooting and trapping the stray elephants for their safety. This condition has become a major threat to the lives of wild elephants

Solutions

We identified some short-term and long-term solutions for this situation

  1. constructing elephant fences between villages and forests.
  2. use a gps collar for wild elephants.
  3. stopping deforestation
  4. setting up elephant orphanages

Our solution is to use a gps collar for wild elephants. It monitors the roaming of wild elephants and sends a message to the villagers when they reach the village. when the message receive before the elephant arrives will enable the villagers to take action. This will enable safety of wild elephants and also humans.


Design information

  1. The collars are strong and secure, with a belt made primarily of natural rubber that does not irritate the skin.
  2. The collars have an electronic module, also known as a "tracker," as well as a counterweight.
  3. The tracker acquires the GPS satellite location and sends it to the server via the local GSM network in seconds (2G, 3G, 4G).
  4. Since the tracker supports 2-way communication, you can adjust the configuration from 1 position per minute to 1 position per 24 hours remotely at any time.
  5. In the event of a failed transmission, the tracker will save up to 20,000 locations, which will be sent when the next link is established. Collars are delivered with a 4.5m length to match even the biggest elephant.
  6. The weight of one elephant collar is just 0.3 to 0.4 percent of an adult elephant's bodyweight. It varies depending on the circumference of the neck.
  7. The tracker is powered by an imported Lithium battery pack with the highest output available today, with a 10-year life span.
  8. When the tracker is in stand-by mode, the battery level decreases by around 3% per year. The number of transmissions every day determines the tracker's real existence.
  9. Every day, the battery level is remotely controlled and available on the gui and smartphone app, enabling you to fine-tune the setup and prolong the battery's life according to the conditions of the monitoring software even during storage times.

How System Works




Since the tracker supports 2-way communication, you can adjust the configuration from 1 position per minute to 1 position per 24 hours remotely at any time. The tracker acquires the GPS satellite location and sends it to the server via the local GSM network in seconds (2G, 3G, 4G). It lets you know where the elephants are and how long they have been in a certain place. When the elephant approaches the village, a notification goes to the Smart Phone.

How to Install Belt



An Elephant must first be selected with the help of the Wildlife Board. The elephant should be then be anesthetized. Once the elephant is anesthetized, the TUSKER TRACKER tracking tape should be wrapped around the elephant's neck. This strap is fastened so as not to damage the elephant and it is very strong.

How to get data from the system



The system receives locations through the gps tracking module. A server computer may be used to save the statistics. Data will be saved in the database, and information gathered during analysis will be saved as well. In the event that data is unable to be transmitted, the tracker has some storage space to save the data before it is possible to transmit it. The information can be accessed at any time by anyone who has access to a computer.

Limitations

  • Normally this should be used for every elephant in the herd, but due to financial and practical difficulties, it has to be used for one elephant in a herd.
  • Having to carry a large initial capital.
  • Having to deal with installation difficulties.
  • Technical and Repair issues.
  • Having to replace the battery from time to time.
  • The coller can be harmful to the elephant.
